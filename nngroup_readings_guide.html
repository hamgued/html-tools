Group 1 — Before you write anything

1
How Users Read on the Web
Jakob Nielsen · 1997 · ~3 min
FoundationalScanning
▾

This is the most important article in the list. Nielsen studied how people actually read websites — and discovered that 79% of users scan rather than read word by word. Only 16% read everything. This completely changes how you should write. If you write long paragraphs with no structure, most readers will miss your message.

↗ Read on nngroup.com
2
Be Succinct! Writing for the Web
Jakob Nielsen · 1997 · ~4 min
FoundationalWord count
▾

People read 25% more slowly on screen than on paper. They also dislike scrolling. Nielsen's advice: write 50% fewer words than you would in a printed document. This does not mean cutting information — it means being direct and starting with the most important point first.

↗ Read on nngroup.com
3
Writing for the Web vs. Writing for Print
Jakob Nielsen · 2008 · ~5 min
FoundationalWeb vs print
▾

Print is linear — the author leads the reader from beginning to end. The web is different: readers arrive at any page, skip around, and pursue their own goals. They are not following your story — they are hunting for specific information. This article explains why web writing is a genuinely different skill, not just "shorter essays".

↗ Read on nngroup.com

Group 2 — Core principles

4
Concise, SCANNABLE, and Objective: How to Write for the Web
Morkes & Nielsen · 1997 · ~12 min
Core researchLonger read
▾

The research study behind the three key principles of web writing. Nielsen and Morkes tested five different versions of the same website and measured how well users could find information. The version that combined concise, scannable, and non-promotional writing scored 124% higher in usability. This is the evidence base for almost everything in this course.

⚠️ This article is longer and more academic than the others. It is worth reading carefully — the before/after text examples are especially useful.

↗ Read on nngroup.com
5
Inverted Pyramids in Cyberspace
Jakob Nielsen · 1996 · ~2 min
Core principleStructure
▾

A very short article with one important idea: start with the conclusion. In traditional essay writing, you build up to the main point. On the web, you do the opposite — give readers the most important information first, then add details below. This structure is called the inverted pyramid. If readers leave early, they still got the key message.

↗ Read on nngroup.com
6
Inverted Pyramid: Writing for Comprehension
Hoa Loranger · 2017 · ~6 min
Core principleStructure
▾

A deeper look at the inverted pyramid structure with practical examples. This article shows how to apply the "conclusion first" principle to web pages of different types — not just news articles. Read this alongside article #5 for a more complete understanding of how to structure your web pages.

↗ Read on nngroup.com

Group 3 — How readers move through pages

7
F-Shaped Pattern of Reading on the Web
Jakob Nielsen · 2006, updated 2017 · ~8 min
Eyetracking researchLayout
▾

Using eyetracking cameras, researchers recorded exactly where people look on a web page. The result: a shape like the letter F. People read across the top, then a shorter line lower down, then scan the left side vertically. Important content placed on the right side or at the bottom is often missed completely. The article also explains that good writing changes the pattern — structured pages with subheadings produce better results.

↗ Read on nngroup.com
8
Text Scanning Patterns: Eyetracking Evidence
Kara Pernice · 2019 · ~8 min
Eyetracking researchScanning patterns
▾

An update to the F-pattern research. Pernice identifies four scanning patterns: the F-pattern (for unformatted text), the spotted pattern (jumping to words that stand out), the layer-cake pattern (scanning only headings), and the commitment pattern (actually reading everything). The layer-cake pattern is the best outcome for a well-structured page. It shows why descriptive subheadings are the most important tool a web writer has.

↗ Read on nngroup.com

Group 4 — Writing at the right length

9
How Much Do Users Read?
Jakob Nielsen · 2008 · ~3 min
Reading behaviourWord count
▾

Short and sobering. On an average web page visit, users read at most 28% of the words — and more likely only 20%. This does not mean you should write almost nothing. It means you should structure your writing so that the 20% users do read contains the most important information. A good test: does your page still make sense if someone only reads the headings and the first sentence of each paragraph?

↗ Read on nngroup.com
10
UX Copy Sizes: Long-Form, Short-Form, and Microcopy
Nielsen Norman Group · 2025 · ~7 min
Content designCopy types
▾

A useful framework: not all web writing is the same length or purpose. Long-form copy (a full page) tells a complete story. Short-form copy (2–3 paragraphs) communicates one idea quickly. Microcopy (under 3 sentences — button labels, captions, short instructions) guides users in the moment. Understanding which type you need helps you decide how much to write. For your capstone website, most pages will use a combination of all three.

↗ Read on nngroup.com
11
Long-Form Articles: Techniques that Improve Comprehension
Nielsen Norman Group · 2023 · ~8 min
Content designLonger content
▾

When a page exceeds 1,000 words, readers need extra help to navigate it. This article covers practical techniques: summaries at the top, callout boxes for key points, strategic use of bold text, and helpful visuals. Directly useful for your Survey Results page, where you need to present data and analysis — the longest and most complex writing task in the capstone.

↗ Read on nngroup.com

Group 5 — Before drafting: language, tone, structure

12
Plain Language Is for Everyone, Even Experts
Pernice & Whitenton · 2017 · ~7 min
LanguagePlain language
▾

Many students think that using complex vocabulary makes writing sound more professional or intelligent. This research shows the opposite: even highly educated professionals — doctors, IT managers — prefer plain, direct language when reading online. Plain language is not about writing for beginners. It is about respecting your reader's time. The article includes real examples and quotes from experts who were tested in usability studies.

↗ Read on nngroup.com
13
The Four Dimensions of Tone of Voice
Kate Moran · 2016 · ~6 min
Tone & voiceStyle
▾

Tone of voice is not just about "formal vs. informal". This article presents a four-part model: humour, formality, respectfulness, and enthusiasm. Every piece of web writing sits somewhere on each of these scales. Understanding this helps you make deliberate choices — especially on your About Us page, where tone defines your brand's personality. A company selling children's toys and a law firm use very different tones, and both can be done well.

↗ Read on nngroup.com
14
Headings Are Pick-Up Lines: 5 Tips for Writing Headlines
Jakob Nielsen · 2015 · ~6 min
HeadingsScannability
▾

Your headings are the first — and sometimes only — thing readers look at. This article explains why headings are so powerful and gives five practical rules: be descriptive, front-load the key word, avoid "clever" or vague headings, keep them short, and use parallel structure when you have multiple headings on the same page. You can check all your capstone page headings against this list before you submit.

↗ Read on nngroup.com

Group 6 — Writing for a specific page type

15
"About Us" Information on Websites
Jakob Nielsen · 2019 · ~13 min
About Us pagesLonger read
▾

Research on what users actually want from an About Us page — and how companies typically fail to provide it. Users want to know: who are you, what do you do, are you trustworthy, and why should I choose you? They compare the About Us page with external reviews and look for signs of authenticity. This article is directly relevant to Step 3 of the capstone (your About Us page) and gives you a practical checklist of what to include.

↗ Read on nngroup.com
16
Tips for Better Product Descriptions on Websites
Nielsen Norman Group · ~8 min
Products pagesE-commerce
▾

Research on what makes product descriptions work — and why so many fail. Nielsen found that 20% of all failed online purchases were caused by incomplete or unclear product descriptions. Users cannot touch, try, or ask questions about a product online, so the text has to do that work. The article gives three core requirements: answer the user's questions, be direct, and help with comparison. Directly relevant to your Products/Services page in the capstone.

↗ Read on nngroup.com
17
Writing to Attract Readers: User-centric vs. Maker-centric Language
Nielsen Norman Group · ~7 min
Products pagesLanguage
▾

A practical article about the difference between writing from the organisation's point of view versus the reader's point of view. Maker-centric language lists features using internal jargon ("Ortholite® insole", "Aegis® antimicrobial treatment"). User-centric language explains the benefit clearly ("comfortable foam insole you can wash", "keeps your shoes fresh longer"). The article shows before-and-after examples of real product descriptions. Directly applicable to both your About Us and Products pages.

↗ Read on nngroup.com
18
Homepage Design: 5 Fundamental Principles
Nielsen Norman Group · 2024 · ~9 min
HomepageSite structure
▾

A homepage has one job: tell visitors who you are, what you offer, and why it matters — fast. This article covers the five principles behind effective homepage design, with a focus on content decisions rather than visual design. Key points include placing important content above the fold, providing specific examples rather than vague category labels, and writing calls to action that describe what actually happens when you click. Useful for thinking about how your Google Sites website presents itself as a whole.
