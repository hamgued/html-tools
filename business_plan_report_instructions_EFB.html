What is this assignment?

📋 Overview and individual work

You will write a Business Plan Report by yourself. You will choose an imaginary business idea, define key business terms, and write a business plan. You will also give and receive peer feedback, use an AI tool in class, and reflect on both types of feedback. At the end of the semester, you will present your business idea at a Business Fair. The whole process runs across the semester.

Individual work: This is an individual assignment. You write your own report. For peer feedback, you will be part of a small peer team. Your team will meet twice on your own to exchange feedback on drafts, and once more in class with your instructor. After the in-class peer session, you will experiment with AI feedback. All writing must be your own.

🎯 Learning goals

Writing and research: Write a structured report and learn how professional writing works in English. Search for good sources online. Cite sources correctly.

Feedback and reflection: Give and receive peer feedback within a small peer team — two self-scheduled meetings and one in-class session. After the in-class peer session, use an AI tool (with your instructor) to get feedback on your writing. Compare peer and AI feedback critically and reflect on what you learned.

Definitions and concepts: Define key business plan terms using sources. Write clear, source-supported definitions in your own words.

Speaking and presenting: Present your business idea at the end-of-semester Business Fair. Practise explaining your idea clearly in English and answering questions from other students.

⚠️ What is plagiarism?

Plagiarism is using someone else's words or ideas without saying where they come from. This includes copying text without a citation, paraphrasing too closely, or submitting AI-generated text as your own. Always write in your own words and cite your sources. If you are not sure, cite — it is better to cite too much than too little.

🚦 Part A: The AI Traffic Light System (Arene AI Traffic Lights)

Your institution uses a four-colour system to show how AI may be used in each task. Click a colour to learn what it means.

🔵 Blue — AI freely permitted. You may use AI tools freely to complete the task. No need to disclose use.

📌 Part B: What colour is this assignment?
🟡 This assignment is YELLOW — you can use AI, but you must report every time you use it.

You may:

  • Use AI to check grammar, spelling, and punctuation
  • Use AI to give feedback on your draft (in class, with your instructor)
  • Use AI to brainstorm ideas — but you must write all final text yourself
  • Use AI to create a logo or brand image for Section 3.1

You must NOT:

  • Ask AI to write any section of your report
  • Copy AI-generated text and submit it as your own
  • Use AI to summarize sources instead of reading them yourself
  • Ask AI to generate a reference list (AI often invents fake sources)

If you use AI, you must:

  • Save screenshots or copies of your AI chat sessions
  • Describe your AI use in Section 5 of your report
  • Complete the AI disclosure form provided by your instructor
🚫 Failing to disclose AI use is treated as fraud and will affect your grade.

📊 Part D: The Writing Spectrum

How much AI use is too much? Tap each level to explore.

Inspired by Jim O'Mahony and Ville Arvola (ITK2025)

Level 1 — No AI use. You wrote everything yourself without any AI assistance. All ideas, phrasing, and structure are entirely your own.
Level 2 — AI for proofreading only. You used AI to catch grammar or spelling errors. The content and ideas are entirely your own.
Level 3 — AI for feedback and brainstorming. You used AI to get feedback on your draft or brainstorm ideas. You evaluated the suggestions critically and rewrote everything in your own words.
Level 4 — Heavy AI assistance. AI significantly shaped the content, structure, or phrasing of your report. You may have edited or paraphrased AI output. This is approaching the boundary of acceptable use.
Level 5 — AI-generated text submitted as your own. The text was generated by AI and submitted without meaningful rewriting. This is academic fraud.
🤖 Part E: Ethical AI Use Tiers

These three tiers describe different types of AI use. Know which tier your activity falls into before you start.

Inspired by Cheng, Calhoun & Reedy 2025

✅ Tier 1 — Acceptable: Using AI as a tool to support your own thinking. Examples: proofreading, getting feedback on a draft you wrote, using AI to create a logo or image, brainstorming ideas you then develop yourself.
⚠️ Tier 2 — Use with care: Using AI in ways that could blur the line between your work and AI output. Examples: asking AI to rewrite a paragraph you wrote, using AI suggestions without critically evaluating them. Always disclose and reflect.
🚫 Tier 3 — Avoid: Using AI in ways that replace your own thinking or writing. Examples: asking AI to write a section, copying AI-generated text, using AI to summarize sources instead of reading them. This is academic fraud.

Step 1 — Learn about AI

This step is not assessed directly, but it helps you avoid mistakes that could affect your grade.

2
Step 2 — Peer Review
Two self-scheduled meetings + one in-class session · Write your reflection in Section 4.1

Peer review happens in three rounds. You write individually, but you are part of a small peer team for feedback purposes.

  • Meeting 1 (self-scheduled, early semester): share your early draft with your peer team and give written feedback on theirs.
  • Meeting 2 (self-scheduled, mid semester): share your first draft and give feedback on your peers' first drafts.
  • Meeting 3 (in class): peer feedback session with your instructor present. AI feedback follows directly after this session.
ℹ️ There are no right or wrong answers in your reflection. Disagreeing with feedback is a valid response. What matters is that you explain your reasoning.

What to write in Section 4.1:

  • What were the main strengths of your text according to your peers?
  • What specific suggestions for improvement did you receive?
  • Did your peers point out problems you had not noticed yourself? Explain.
  • How did the feedback confirm or challenge your own view of your writing?
  • What changes did you make — and were there suggestions you chose not to act on? Why?

Length: 2–3 paragraphs, approximately 60–75 words each. Write as continuous text, not a bullet list.

3
Step 3 — AI Feedback
In class, after the peer session · Write your reflection in Section 4.2

This takes place in class, immediately after the in-class peer feedback session, with your instructor's guidance. You will use an AI feedback tool to get feedback on your draft.

What to write in Section 4.2:

  • What kind of feedback did the AI mainly give you?
  • Which parts were helpful or accurate, and why?
  • Did the feedback feel general or specific to your text? Give an example.
  • Did you notice any errors or misleading suggestions? Explain.
  • How did you decide which suggestions to accept, modify, or ignore?
  • Overall, how useful was the AI feedback?

Screenshots: Add a few screenshots of the AI feedback. Label and number them as images.

4
Step 4 — Compare and Reflect
Mid semester onwards · Write Section 4.3

Write 2–3 paragraphs (~60–75 words each) comparing the peer feedback and the AI feedback you received.

Guiding questions for Section 4.3:

  • In what ways was peer feedback different from AI feedback?
  • Which type helped you more with content and ideas, and why?
  • Which type helped you more with language or clarity?
  • Did the two types of feedback ever contradict each other? How did you handle this?
  • What does this tell you about the strengths and limitations of AI in writing?

How to write a definition paragraph
  1. First sentence — introduce the term and say why it is important
  2. Definition — explain what the term means, using a source
  3. Example or explanation — add an example or more detail to help the reader understand
How to cite a source in your text

When you use information from a source, say where it comes from. You can do this in two ways:

Option 1 — name in the sentence:
According to Smith (2024), a value proposition explains the main benefit a business offers its customers.

Option 2 — name at the end:
A value proposition explains the main benefit a business offers its customers (Smith 2024).

How to write your reference list

At the end of your report, list all the sources you used. Use this format:

Last name, First initial. Year. Title of the source. Cited on [date]. [link or website address]

Example:
Smith, A. 2026. What is a business model canvas? Cited on 15 March 2026. https://www.example.com/business-model

You need at least one source for each paragraph in Section 2.

The Business Fair takes place at the end of the semester. Every student takes part twice — once as a presenter and once as a business partner. The roles switch halfway through the class.

🏪 What is the Business Fair?

The Business Fair is like a small trade fair in your classroom. Half of the students stand at their own "stand" and talk about their business idea. The other half walk around and listen, ask questions, and give feedback — like interested business partners or investors.

Halfway through, the roles switch. The presenters become the business partners, and the business partners become the presenters.

You will prepare up to three PowerPoint slides to help you talk about your idea. The slides are a support — not a script. You talk to your visitors naturally, as if you are really trying to convince someone to be your business partner.

✅ The Business Fair is pass/fail. You pass by taking part actively — both as a presenter and as a business partner.
🎤 Your role: Presenter

When you are the presenter, you stand at your stand and talk to the business partners who visit you. You do not need to memorise a speech. Just be ready to talk about your business idea in a clear and friendly way.

Be ready to explain:

  • What is your business idea? What problem does it solve?
  • Who are your customers?
  • How does your business make money?
  • Why is your idea better than other options?
  • What do you need to make it work? (resources, partners)
💡 Tip: You can bring your report, notes, or a printed image of your business model canvas to refer to. You do not need to memorise anything.
🤝 Your role: Business Partner

When you are the business partner, you walk around and visit other students' stands. You listen to their business idea, ask questions, and fill in a short feedback form for each student you visit.

Your job is to:

  • Listen carefully to the presenter
  • Ask at least one question (see examples below)
  • Fill in the feedback form honestly

Example questions you can ask:

  • "Who is your main customer? Can you give me an example?"
  • "How is your business different from similar businesses?"
  • "What is the biggest challenge for your business?"
  • "How much would a customer pay for this?"
  • "What would you need from a business partner like me?"
💡 Tip: You do not need to be an expert. Just be curious and ask questions you are genuinely interested in.
📋 Feedback Form

Fill in one form for each presenter you visit. Be specific and honest — your feedback helps the presenter improve.

Print one copy per presenter you visit, or ask your instructor for printed copies.

✅ How are you assessed?

The Business Fair is pass/fail. It is not included in your report grade.

You pass if you:

  • Are present for the full session
  • Present your business idea as a presenter
  • Visit other students and fill in feedback forms as a business partner
  • Communicate in English throughout
⚠️ If you are absent or do not take part, you will get a fail, and your instructor may lower your final report grade. How much depends on the situation — your instructor will decide.
💡 How to prepare

You need to prepare a short PowerPoint presentation with a maximum of three slides. The slides are there to help you talk — not to read from. Keep them simple: a few words or an image per slide.

Suggested slide structure:

  • Slide 1 — Your business idea: Name of the business, what it does, and who it is for
  • Slide 2 — Why it is a good idea: Your value proposition and what makes it different
  • Slide 3 — How it works: How you make money, who your key partners are, and what you need to get started

Read through your business plan before the fair and practise explaining it in simple English. Think about what questions you might get and how you would answer them.

💬 Useful phrases:
"My business idea is…"
"The main customer is… because…"
"We make money by…"
"The biggest challenge is… but we plan to…"
"That's a great question. I think…"
