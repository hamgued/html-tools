Writing Your Academic Report

What is this assignment?
You and your team will write a short academic report together. You choose one of two topics, follow a fixed template structure, find and cite credible sources, go through at least one draft, and submit a final version before the deadline. The whole process — choosing a topic, researching, drafting, getting feedback, and revising — runs across the semester.
Learning goals

Write a structured academic report and learn the conventions of academic communication. Search for credible sources through the library database (LAB Primo) and cite them correctly following LAB's Thesis Guidebook format.

Practice collaborative writing as a team. Give and receive peer feedback on a team's draft. Use AI tools (within the allowed limits) to get proofreading feedback, and reflect on the difference between peer and AI feedback.

Define 8 key terms related to your chosen topic (business plan or UX research) and write clear, source-supported definitions for each one.

Paragraph structure for each definition
Every definition subsection should follow this three-part structure:
  1. Topic sentence — introduce the term and explain why it matters in your topic area.
  2. Definition — define the term using a credible source, cited correctly.
  3. Remaining paragraph — elaborate with examples, additional sources, or further explanation.

A strong author citation puts the author's name in the sentence itself. This is the preferred style — it gives more weight to your source and reads more academically. There are three patterns:

Pattern 1 — "according to"
…according to Payne et al. (2017, 467), value proposition is a strategic tool that a business can use to outline the benefits it offers and the value it creates.
Pattern 2 — reporting verb (explain, describe, note…)
…Payne et al. (2017, 467) explain that value proposition is a strategic tool that a business can use to outline the benefits it offers and the value it creates.
Pattern 3 — "define … as"
…Payne et al. (2017, 467) define value proposition as a strategic tool that a business can use to outline the benefits it offers and the value it creates.

You can also acknowledge disagreement or multiple definitions before your chosen one: "While there are many definitions and some disagreement (Kaikkonen 2017, 34), according to Payne et al. (2017, 467)…"

A weak author citation places the author in brackets at the end of the sentence. This is acceptable but less emphatic. Use it when the idea matters more than who said it.

Pattern 1 — passive voice + brackets
Usability can be defined as part of UX research and a core concept in discussions on better user experience (Smith & Wesson 2022, 24).
Pattern 2 — "is" + brackets
Usability is part of UX research and a core concept in discussions on better user experience (Smith & Wesson 2022, 24).

Every source you cite must appear in the references list at the end of the report. Follow LAB's Thesis Guidebook format (chapters 7.2–7.4). Here are examples of correctly formatted references:

Hassan, M. M. & Tabasum, M. 2018. Customer profiling and segmentation in retail banks using data mining techniques. International journal of advanced research in computer science. Vol. 9 (4), 24–29. Retrieved on [date]. Available at DOI 10.26483/ijarcs.v9i4.6172.

Payne, A., Frow, P. & Eggert, A. 2017. The customer value proposition: evolution, development, and application in marketing. Journal of the Academy of Marketing Science, Vol 45 (4), 467–489. Retrieved on [date]. Available at DOI 10.1007/s11747-017-0523-z.

Smith, B. & Wesson, J. 2022. UX Research Essentials. E-book. UX Research Publishers. LAB Primo.

Note: include the retrieval date and URL or DOI for online sources. For e-books found through LAB Primo, add "LAB Primo" at the end.

Formatting & sources

Use LAB Primo (the library database) to search for academic books and journal articles. Avoid using Wikipedia, random websites, or AI-generated text as sources.

Each definition in section 2 needs at least one source. Section 3 should also include cited sources where appropriate. Aim for a mix of source types.

Follow LAB's Thesis Guidebook, chapters 6.1, 6.2, 7.2, 7.3, and 7.4 for in-text citations and reference list formatting.

Every source cited in the text must appear in the reference list, and every item in the reference list must be cited in the text.

The template includes placeholders for images, figures, and tables. Label all visuals (e.g. "Figure 1. Our user journey map") and refer to them in your text. For section 3 in the business plan template, there is a placeholder for a brand image and a customer table. For the UX template, there is a user persona table and a journey map figure.

Mid semester

What counts as a first draft?
All template sections are present and contain real content (mostly complete). Perfect grammar, spelling, and formatting are not required at this stage — errors are normal and expected. You can use placeholders like TBC (to be completed), NSS (needs a source), or FCN (further clarification needed).
The purpose of the first draft is to have something concrete to review, critique, and improve — and to prepare you to write the abstract in the midterm exam.
How to submit
No late submissions. The submission link closes automatically at the deadline. Plan ahead.
1
Use the correct template. Template 1 for the business plan, Template 2 for UX research. Do not rename sections or change the structure.
2
Only the team leader uploads. One person submits on behalf of the whole group. Everyone else should make sure the leader has the latest version well before the deadline.
3
Upload draft versions early. Submit drafts to get a Turnitin Similarity Report. Turnitin can take several hours or up to a day to respond — don't wait until the last minute.
4
Review and revise. If Turnitin flags high similarity, edit your report and resubmit. Keep refining until you are satisfied with the final version.
5
Submit your final version before the deadline. Make sure the uploaded file is your best, complete version. You cannot make changes after the link closes.
Turnitin & plagiarism

Turnitin is a tool that checks your report for similarity with other texts and flags potential plagiarism. It also includes AI writing detection. The Similarity Report shows which parts of your text match other sources.

A high similarity score does not automatically mean plagiarism — properly quoted and cited text will show as similar. But you should review the report and paraphrase or cite anything that looks like it was copied.

Plagiarism is using someone else's words or ideas without proper attribution. This includes copying text from a source without citation, paraphrasing too closely without a reference, or submitting AI-generated content as your own work.

Always write in your own words and cite your sources. If in doubt, cite — over-citing is better than under-citing.

Rules for using AI tools
Not allowed: You may NOT use AI tools to write any part of your report. This includes generating definitions, paragraphs, analysis, or any other content.
Allowed: You MAY use AI tools only to proofread — checking grammar, spelling, and punctuation. No rewriting.
Allowed: You MAY use AI to get feedback on your writing — but this is done together with your instructor in class, not independently.
If you use AI, you must
1
Save records. Take screenshots or save copies of your AI chat sessions for every time you used an AI tool.
2
Disclose it in section 5. Fill in the AI use disclosure section of your report. Failing to disclose AI use is treated as fraud and will affect your grade.
3
Complete the AI disclosure form provided by your instructor (linked in the course).
Recommended proofreading prompt
Copy and paste this into your AI tool of choice
Role: You are a proofreading tool.
Focus area: Proofread for grammar, spelling, and punctuation only.
Boundaries: Do not rewrite anything. Respect my original authorial voice and only flag objective writing mistakes.
Context: I am writing an academic report.
Feedback format: Provide your feedback in bullet points.
How to start: Wait for me to give you a passage or file to check.
AI in the classroom
Your instructor will use AI tools together with you in class — specifically for getting AI feedback on your writing, which goes into section 4.2 of your report. In section 4.3, you reflect on how the peer feedback and AI feedback compared. Keep notes on both.
