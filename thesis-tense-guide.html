Which Tense and Why?

Theses and Research Papers

Designed by Hamid Guedra  ·  LAB University of Applied Sciences

In your introduction, you describe your topic and your goals. Use present tense because these things are still true when the reader reads your text.

Primary Present tense

Use present tense when you describe what your thesis is about and what you want to find out. These facts are still true now, so you use present tense.

Examples

This thesis examines digital literacy among first-year university students.
The study aims to identify key barriers to academic writing.
The research question concerns how written feedback affects revision behaviour.
This paper investigates the relationship between sleep and academic performance.
The purpose of this thesis is to explore how social media influences consumer behaviour.
This study focuses on the challenges faced by international students in Finnish universities.
Optional Future or present tense

You can use future or present tense to tell the reader what each chapter or section covers. Both are correct. This helps the reader understand the structure of your thesis.

Future tense

Chapter 2 will present the theoretical framework.
The following section will outline the research design.
Chapter 4 will discuss the main findings of the study.
The conclusion will summarise the key results and suggest directions for future research.

Present tense — also correct

Chapter 2 presents the theoretical framework.
The following section outlines the research design.
Chapter 4 discusses the main findings of the study.
The conclusion summarises the key results and suggests directions for future research.
💡 Watch out: Do not write "This thesis aimed to…" in the introduction. Use past tense like this only in the conclusion, when you look back at what you did.

In the theoretical background section, you can use three different tenses. You can even use all three in the same paragraph. The tense you choose shows how you are talking about the source.

For specific past studies Past tense

Use past tense when you describe a specific study that happened in the past. You usually mention the year next to the author's name.

Examples

Smith (2018) found that motivation declined significantly in online courses.
Garcia (2019) reported a significant correlation between feedback frequency and revision quality.
Johnson and Lee (2020) examined how peer feedback affected student writing.
Chen and Park (2021) conducted interviews with 45 university students.
Brown (2015) concluded that self-assessment improved learning outcomes.
For author arguments & claims Present tense

Use present tense when you describe what an author thinks or believes. Their ideas are still relevant today, so you use present tense.

Examples

Smith (2018) argues that autonomy plays a central role in language learning.
The framework distinguishes between surface and deep approaches to learning (Biggs 1999).
Johnson (2020) claims that written feedback is more effective than oral feedback.
Brown and Lee (2022) suggest that motivation depends on both internal and external factors.
The model identifies three key stages in the writing process (Garcia 2019).
For research trends Present perfect

Use present perfect when you want to show that something started in the past and is still important today. It connects older research to the current situation in your field.

Examples

Researchers have increasingly focused on the role of peer feedback in academic writing (Smith 2018; Johnson 2020).
Several studies have demonstrated a positive effect of formative assessment on student outcomes (Brown 2015; Garcia 2019).
Interest in online learning has grown significantly over the past decade (Chen & Park 2021).
Scholars have debated the definition of motivation for many years (Brown & Lee 2022).
Recent research has shown that feedback timing matters as much as feedback quality (Johnson 2020).
ℹ️ It is correct — and normal — to use all three tenses in the same paragraph. Example: "Smith (2018) found [past] that motivation declined — and this remains [present] a key issue today, as more recent studies have confirmed [present perfect]."

In this section, you describe what you did and what you found. Use past tense because your research is now finished.

Dominant throughout Past tense

Everything in this section is finished, so use past tense. This applies to both what you did (your methods) and what you found (your results).

Methods

Data were collected through semi-structured interviews with 12 participants.
Participants were recruited using purposive sampling.
A questionnaire was distributed to 80 students in three different classes.
The interviews lasted approximately 30 minutes each.
All responses were recorded and later transcribed for analysis.

Results

The analysis revealed three recurring themes across the data.
Response rates varied considerably across participant groups.
Most participants reported a preference for written feedback.
The results showed a clear difference between the two groups.
Over 70% of respondents agreed that peer feedback was helpful.
A note on voice Passive voice — common but not required

Many writers use passive voice in this section (e.g. "Data were collected"). This puts the focus on the action, not on you as the researcher. But you can also write "I collected data" — both are correct. Ask your supervisor which they prefer.

Passive vs active — same meaning

Transcripts were coded thematically. (passive)
I coded the transcripts thematically. (active)
The data were analysed using thematic analysis. (passive)
I analysed the data using thematic analysis. (active)
Participants were asked to complete a short questionnaire. (passive)
I asked participants to complete a short questionnaire. (active)
⚠️ Only use present tense in this section when you refer to a table or figure. For example: "Table 1 shows the results." For everything else, use past tense.

In the discussion, you do two things: you talk about your results (past tense) and you explain what they mean (present tense). Changing tense here is correct — it has a purpose.

Referring back to findings Past tense

Use past tense when you refer back to what your data showed. Your research is finished, so you describe the results in the past.

Examples

The results indicated a strong preference for written over oral feedback.
Participants reported feeling unsupported during the revision stage.
The data showed that younger students used social media more frequently.
Most interviewees mentioned time pressure as a key challenge.
The survey revealed that 65% of respondents preferred online learning.
Interpreting meaning Present tense

Use present tense when you explain what your results mean. You are sharing your conclusions and ideas, which are current and still relevant.

Examples

This suggests that feedback timing matters more than feedback volume.
The pattern aligns with what Smith (2018) found in a similar context.
These findings challenge the assumptions made by Garcia (2019) about autonomous learning.
This indicates that students need more structured support during the writing process.
The result supports what Brown and Lee (2022) argue about the value of peer feedback.
This confirms the findings of Chen and Park (2021) on online motivation.
✅ A common pattern in the discussion:
"The interviews showed that students had problems with citations [past] — this suggests that more support is needed [present]."
Changing tense here is correct. Each tense has a different job.

In the conclusion, you summarise what you found and what it means. You also suggest what could be studied or done next.

Summarising main points Present tense

Use present tense to tell the reader what your thesis shows. These are your main conclusions, and they are still true now.

Examples

The findings demonstrate the value of formative feedback in academic writing contexts.
This thesis contributes to a better understanding of peer review practices in higher education.
The results show that regular feedback has a positive effect on student writing.
This study highlights the importance of early intervention in the writing process.
The evidence supports the use of peer review as a regular classroom activity.
Recommendations & future work Future / modal tense

Use future tense or modal verbs (could, may, might) when you suggest what researchers or practitioners could do next.

Examples

Future studies could explore the long-term effects of peer feedback on writing development.
Teachers may wish to consider using peer review at different stages of the writing process.
Further research should investigate how feedback affects different age groups.
Future studies might focus on the use of AI tools in academic writing courses.
This approach could be applied in other subject areas beyond academic writing.
💡 The conclusion is the one place where you can use past tense to look back at your aims: "This thesis aimed to… and found that…" — followed immediately by a present-tense statement: "These results suggest that…"

A summary of tense choices across all five sections, plus the key principles to keep in mind.

📋 Tense summary by section
Section Tense(s) What each tense is for
1 · Introduction Present Future Present: topic, context, aims.
Future (optional): chapter roadmap.
2 · Theoretical Background Past Present Present perfect Past: specific studies as events.
Present: author arguments and claims.
Present perfect: research trends.
3 · Empirical Part Past (dominant) Reporting completed methods and results. Passive voice common but not required.
4 · Discussion Past Present Past: referring back to findings.
Present: interpreting meaning and significance.
5 · Conclusion Present Future / Modal Present: main points and contributions.
Future/modal: recommendations and further research.

Key principles

🔀 Mixing tenses is normal

When the communicative function changes — e.g. from reporting a result to interpreting it — the tense changes too. That is not inconsistency; it is precision. The key is that every tense shift should have a reason.

😵 Unmotivated switching is the problem

Changing tense with no change in function makes readers lose their footing. Before switching tense, ask yourself: Why am I switching here? If you cannot answer, stay in the same tense.

🎓 Discipline conventions vary

These patterns reflect common academic English conventions. Always check what your field or supervisor expects — some disciplines have stricter norms than others, and conventions in sciences may differ from those in the humanities or social sciences.

✍️ Passive voice is a choice, not a rule

Use passive voice when the agent (who did something) is less important than the action itself. Active voice is equally valid and is often preferred in qualitative or reflective research. Conventions vary by discipline and supervisor.
