Creating A Research Space — a step-by-step guide to writing strong introductions for academic papers and research articles, based on John M. Swales' three-move model.

The CARS model (Create A Research Space) was developed by linguist John M. Swales in 1990 to describe how writers introduce research articles in English. It identifies three rhetorical moves that guide a reader from a broad topic into the specific gap your study addresses.

💡 Think of it as a funnel: you start wide (your field), narrow down to a problem nobody has solved (the niche), then plant your flag on it (your study).

Why it matters for your writing

A weak introduction often fails because it skips Move 2. The writer describes the topic and then jumps straight to "the aim of this paper is…". The reader is left wondering: why does this study need to exist? The CARS model forces you to answer that question explicitly by naming a gap before claiming it.

🎓 The model was developed for research articles, but the same funnel logic works for any academic writing where you need to motivate a study or argument — essays, term papers, literature reviews, and reports.

Move 1 — Establishing a territory. You position your topic in a wider field and convince the reader it deserves attention. This is your widest point in the funnel.

Step 1 — Claiming centrality

You assert that the topic is important, problematic, or relevant — to society, to the discipline, or to a specific group of people. This is your "hook".

Examples

Climate change has become one of the most pressing challenges facing modern societies.
In recent years, there has been growing interest in the use of AI tools in higher education.

Useful language

…has become a major concern · is widely recognised as · plays a key role in · is increasingly important for · has attracted considerable attention

Step 2 — Making topic generalisations

You give the reader background knowledge: established facts, current understanding, or accepted definitions. This is the "what we already know" section.

Examples

Renewable energy sources currently account for approximately 30% of global electricity generation.
It is generally accepted that formative feedback improves student writing outcomes.

Useful language

It is well established that… · Recent studies suggest that… · A common assumption is that… · Generally speaking…

Step 3 — Reviewing items of previous research

You cite specific studies that have addressed the topic. Be selective — only the most relevant work belongs in the introduction. The full literature review comes later.

Examples

Smith and Johnson (2021) demonstrated that peer review improves student engagement in EFL classrooms.
Several studies have examined the impact of remote work on productivity (Lee, 2020; Martinez et al., 2022).

Useful language

X (2023) showed that… · Previous research has demonstrated… · A number of studies have investigated…

⚠️ Common mistake: writing a Move 1 that is so general it could open any paper in the field. Be specific enough that the reader already senses where you are heading.

Move 2 — Establishing a niche. This is the pivot of the introduction. You show that, despite all the work in Move 1, something is still missing, contested, or underexplored. Without Move 2, your study has no reason to exist.

Option A — Counter-claiming

You challenge or push back against an existing claim, theory, or approach. Strong but risky — make sure you can back it up.

However, this assumption has been challenged by recent evidence suggesting the opposite effect.

However… · In contrast… · This view, however, overlooks… · Yet such accounts fail to consider…

Option B — Indicating a gap (most common)

You point out that something has not yet been studied, or has been studied only in limited contexts. This is the most common Move 2 strategy in student writing.

However, little research has been conducted on how these tools affect non-native English writers in vocational higher education.
To date, no study has examined the combined effect of these two variables in a Finnish context.

However, little is known about… · Few studies have addressed… · Research on X remains limited… · The literature has paid less attention to…

Option C — Question-raising

You raise a question that previous research has not answered. Useful when the gap is hard to state as a flat absence.

It remains unclear whether these results would hold in a multilingual classroom.

It remains unclear whether… · An open question is… · Why X happens is still poorly understood…

Option D — Continuing a tradition

You frame your study as the next logical step in an existing line of research. Useful when there is no real gap, only an opportunity to extend prior work.

Building on the framework developed by Swales (1990), this study extends the model to a new academic context.

Building on… · Extending this line of research… · Following the approach of…

🚫 Avoid: "There is no research on X" — this is almost always false and easy for a supervisor to disprove. Use "little research" or "limited research" instead.

Move 3 — Occupying the niche. You step into the gap you just opened. You announce what your study does, what it found, and how the rest of the paper is organised.

Step 1 — Outlining purposes or announcing research

State the aim, objectives, research questions, or hypotheses of your study. This is the most essential element — never omit it.

The aim of this paper is to investigate how vocational higher education students in Finland use generative AI tools when writing academic English.
This study addresses three research questions: (1) … (2) … (3) …

The aim of this study is to… · This paper investigates… · The present research seeks to…

Step 2 — Announcing principal findings (optional)

Common in research articles, less common in shorter student papers. If your assignment expects it, give a one-sentence preview of the main result.

The findings suggest that structured AI prompting significantly improves first-draft quality without reducing student ownership.

The findings suggest that… · Results indicate… · This study shows that…

Step 3 — Indicating the structure of the paper

Give the reader a roadmap of what is coming next. In a longer paper, this often takes a short paragraph at the end of the introduction.

The paper is organised as follows. Section 2 reviews the literature on… Section 3 describes the methodology… Section 4 presents the findings… Section 5 discusses the implications.

This paper is organised as follows… · The remainder of this article proceeds as follows… · Section 2 outlines…

✅ Tip: Move 3 should clearly answer the gap you raised in Move 2. If your aim does not address the gap, one of the two needs to be rewritten.

Generative AI tools such as ChatGPT have rapidly become part of the everyday writing practices of university students across Europe. Surveys conducted in 2023 and 2024 indicate that more than 60% of higher education students now use these tools at some stage of their academic writing process. Recent studies have examined how AI affects student engagement (Lee, 2023), academic integrity (Martinez & Singh, 2024), and the development of writing skills (Park, 2024).

However, little research has so far addressed how non-native English speakers in vocational higher education actually use these tools when drafting academic texts in English. It also remains unclear whether reliance on AI tools supports or undermines the long-term development of independent writing skills in this group.

The aim of this paper is to investigate how first-year business students at a Finnish university of applied sciences use generative AI tools when producing written assignments in English. The paper is structured as follows: Section 2 reviews the literature on AI and academic writing, Section 3 describes the survey methodology, Section 4 reports the findings, and Section 5 discusses pedagogical implications for English teachers at LAB.

💡 Notice how Move 2 directly motivates Move 3: the gap is "non-native speakers in vocational HE" → the aim is "first-year business students at a Finnish UAS". The two halves match.
