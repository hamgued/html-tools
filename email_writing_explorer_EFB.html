📌 Subject line

A good subject line tells the reader exactly what the email is about before they open it. Include the job title and your name so it is easy to identify.

"Application — Marketing Trainee — Aleksi Virtanen"
"Job application: Customer Service role — Maria Korhonen"
"Re: Sales Assistant vacancy — cover letter enclosed"

Dear Hiring Team,

👋 Greeting

Use Dear + a name or title. If you do not know the person's name, Dear Hiring Team or Dear Hiring Manager is correct. Avoid To Whom It May Concern — it sounds old-fashioned.

Not sure of the person's gender? If the name is unfamiliar or from another culture and you cannot tell whether the person uses Mr or Ms, avoid guessing. Use their full name instead — Dear Alex Kim, — or use a neutral title such as Dear Hiring Manager. This is always safe and professional.

"Dear Ms Leinonen," (you know she uses Ms)
"Dear Alex Kim," (full name — safe when gender is unclear)
"Dear Hiring Manager," (no name known)
"Dear GreenLoop Recruitment Team," (addressing a team)

▶️ Opening paragraph

Your first sentence should say who you are and why you are writing. Mention where you saw the job advertisement if possible. Keep it to 2–3 sentences.

"I am writing to express my interest in the Customer Service Trainee role at GreenLoop."
"I would like to apply for the position of Marketing Assistant, as advertised on LinkedIn."
Skills paragraph
I have experience working in a customer-facing role at a local café, where I developed my communication and teamwork skills. In my studies, I have also practised professional writing in English and completed a business plan project that included market research and customer segmentation.

💼 Skills paragraph

Describe 2–3 relevant skills. For each one, give a short example — do not just list skills. Use this structure: skill + example + result or learning.

"I am a reliable team player. During a group project, I coordinated communication and made sure we met our deadline."
"My written English skills are strong, and I have practised writing formal reports and emails during my Business English course."

🤝 Closing paragraph

End by expressing interest in the next step. Mention any attachments. Keep it short — 2–3 sentences maximum.

"I am available for an interview at your convenience and look forward to hearing from you."
"Thank you for considering my application. I hope to have the opportunity to discuss it with you."

✍️ Sign-off

Kind regards and Best regards are the most common sign-offs in modern professional email — use them in almost all situations. Yours sincerely and Yours faithfully are still correct in very formal letters, but they are old-fashioned in everyday business email. Always include your full name and contact details below the sign-off.

"Kind regards, [Full name]" — most common in professional email
"Best regards, [Full name]" — equally common, slightly warmer
"Best wishes, [Full name]" — friendly and professional; good for ongoing relationships
"Yours sincerely, [Full name]" — very formal; correct but old-fashioned for email

✅ Do and ❌ Don't

✅ Do

  • Use full sentences — no contractions (write I am, not I'm)
  • Keep the email between 100–150 words for a cover letter; other emails can be longer
  • Use a clear, specific subject line
  • Start by saying why you are writing
  • Give examples for your skills — not just claims
  • Proofread before sending

❌ Don't

  • Use slang or informal language (Hey, Thanks a lot, FYI)
  • Use contractions (I'm, I've, can't, don't)
  • Write very long paragraphs — keep them short
  • Leave the subject line blank or vague (Hello, Hi)
  • Forget to include your name and contact details
  • Copy from AI without reading and checking carefully
  • Begin with I hope this email finds you well — overused and empty
What is register? Register means the level of formality in your writing. Professional emails use a formal or semi-formal register. Informal language — like contractions and slang — is not appropriate. Use this page to learn the difference.

Contractions — never use them in formal emails

A contraction joins two words with an apostrophe. For example, I'm = I am. In professional emails, always write the full form.

❌ Do not use
I'm · I've · I'd · I'll · can't · don't · won't · it's · there's · we're · you're · haven't · wouldn't · couldn't · didn't
✅ Always write the full form
I am · I have · I would · I will · cannot · do not · will not · it is · there is · we are · you are · have not · would not · could not · did not
