Reporting Verbs Master

Reporting verbs signal your academic stance towards a source. Are you being cautious? Making a firm argument? Or simply reporting an observation? The verb you choose tells the reader.

🟡 Tentative

Used when the writer is being hesitant or speculative — not 100% certain of the claim.

suggestspeculate implypropose hypothesizepostulate recommendmoot intimate

🔵 Neutral

Used to describe facts, methodology, or observations without making a judgment.

describeshow revealnote point outindicate reportobserve stateexamine

🟢 Strong

Used when the writer is making a firm claim or is prepared to defend their argument.

argueclaim emphasizecontend maintainassert refutereject challengedeny

Verb categories in context

Tentative suggest · hypothesize · imply · propose

Use these when the evidence is preliminary or when the author is being cautious about their claim. They signal appropriate academic hedging.

Examples

Smith (2021) suggests that remote work may improve productivity in certain contexts.
The data implies a link between sleep deprivation and poor academic performance.
The team hypothesizes a new mechanism for dark matter distribution.
The researcher proposes that early intervention could reduce dropout rates significantly.
Neutral describe · indicate · note · show · report

Use these to report findings, observations, or methodology without adding a stance. These are the most frequent verbs in academic writing.

Examples

The study describes how temperature affects the rate of the chemical reaction.
The latest figures indicate a significant increase in urban migration.
Johnson (2019) notes that feedback frequency varies considerably across institutions.
The survey reveals a strong preference for online learning among respondents.
Strong argue · contend · refute · assert · challenge

Use these when the author is making a firm, defensible claim, or actively disagreeing with an existing position.

Examples

Miller (2020) argues that the current economic model is fundamentally unsustainable.
Dr. Aris refutes the long-held theory that taxes always reduce economic growth.
The author challenges the widely held view that grammar instruction is unnecessary.
She maintains that the original findings remain valid despite recent criticism.

Grammar rules

⚠️
Two rules to master
These are the two most common grammar errors students make when using reporting verbs in academic writing.

1. The Third-Person -s

When the subject is singular (e.g., Smith, the author, the study), add -s to the verb.

✅ Smith argues…
❌ Smith argue…
✅ The study shows…
❌ The study show…

2. Present Tense Choice

Use Present Simple to bring past research into the current academic conversation.

✅ Jones suggests that…
❌ Jones suggested that…
✅ The data show that…
💡 Exception: Use past tense when you cite a specific completed study — e.g., "Smith (2018) found that…" — especially when the year appears next to the author's name. This treats the research as a completed event in time.
