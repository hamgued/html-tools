1How to Use This Guide

This guide covers what you need to write your thesis in English at LAB. Use the menu on the left to jump to any topic. The last section (Common Feedback) lists the most frequent mistakes found in student theses, with before-and-after examples.

This guide is based on the LAB University of Applied Sciences Thesis Guidebook (August 2025 edition). If you are unsure about something, always check the original guidebook on eLAB.

💡
Always write your thesis in the LAB thesis template in Microsoft Word. The template already has the correct styles, margins, and font settings. Do not change these.
How Your Writing Is Graded

Your language instructor will grade your writing on five areas.

AreaWhat it meansHow to meet it
1. StructureYour chapters, sections, and paragraphs are organised clearly and logically.Use the LAB template. Follow the required section order.
2. Fluency & StyleYour writing is clear, professional, and easy to read.Avoid informal words. Write and proofread carefully.
3. Grammatical AccuracyYour grammar is correct. You use the right tenses. Your spelling is consistent.Use Word's Editor (set to English UK or US — be consistent throughout).
4. ReferencingYou follow LAB referencing rules in your citations and reference list.Follow Thesis Guidebook chapters 7.3 and 7.4. Every source cited must appear in the list.
5. VisualsAll tables, figures, and images are numbered, labelled, and discussed.Refer to every visual in the text before it appears. Always analyse what it shows.

2Thesis Structure

Most LAB bachelor's theses have this structure. The parts must appear in the right order. The LAB thesis template handles the formatting.

—
Statement about the Use of AI
Required. Explains whether you used AI tools and how.
—
Abstract
A short summary: your topic, methods, key findings, and 3–7 keywords.
—
Table of Contents
Created automatically from the LAB Heading styles. Never typed by hand.
—
List of Abbreviations / Terms
Only needed if your thesis has many field-specific terms or abbreviations.
1
Introduction
Background, objectives, research questions, and delimitations.
2–5
Main Body Chapters
Theoretical framework, case company or context, empirical/research chapters, analysis.
6–7
Summary and Discussion
Revisits your objectives, summarises findings, and suggests future research.
—
References
All sources you cited, in alphabetical order.
—
Appendices
Questionnaires, large tables, and other extra material. Only if needed.
⚠️
Each chapter must have at least two paragraphs. Each paragraph must have at least two sentences. A chapter cannot be only lists or visuals.
Abstract

The abstract is a short, independent summary of your thesis. It answers three questions:

  • What did you study and why?
  • How did you do it (what methods did you use)?
  • What are the key findings and conclusions?
RuleExample
Use past tense for backgroundThe thesis studied the following…
Use present tense for conclusionsThe findings show that…
Use impersonal language — write about the thesis, not "I" or "we"The thesis investigates… (not: "We investigated…")
Do not refer back to the thesis with "this" — the abstract is a separate textThe thesis investigates… (not: "In this thesis…")
List 3–7 keywords at the endKeywords: supply chain, resilience, Finland
Introduction

The introduction sets up your whole thesis. It should include:

  • Background — what is the topic and why does it matter?
  • Objectives and delimitations — what does your thesis aim to do? What does it not cover?
  • Research questions — one main question, plus supporting sub-questions
  • Method overview — a brief description of how you collected and analysed data
  • Chapter roadmap (optional) — tells the reader how the report is structured
Open vs. Closed Research Questions
❌ Closed — avoidDoes Company X's marketing plan increase sales?
✅ Open — betterHow could Company X's marketing plan be developed to increase sales among Gen Z?
Method vs. Methodology
❌ VagueThe research was conducted using qualitative method.
✅ SpecificThe research is qualitative. Data was collected through semi-structured interviews.
💡
Method = the tool you used to collect data (e.g., semi-structured interview). Methodology = the theoretical or philosophical framework underlying your research approach (e.g., interpretivism, positivism, action research). In most cases, use the word method.
Main Body Chapters
Heading rules
  • If a chapter has sub-sections (e.g., 3.1), do not write any body text between the chapter heading (3) and the first sub-section.
  • A sub-section at level 2 (e.g., 3.1) must have at least one full paragraph before any level 3 sub-heading (e.g., 3.1.1).
  • You must always have at least two sub-sections at the same level. If you have 3.1, you need 3.2.
  • Heading numbers do not end with a full stop. Write 3.1, not 3.1.
  • Do not use question marks or exclamation marks in headings.
  • Be consistent: use either title case or sentence case throughout — do not mix both.
ℹ️
Use Theoretical Framework or Theoretical Background, not Knowledge Base. "Knowledge base" is a direct translation from Finnish ("tietoperusta") but not standard in English academic writing.
Summary & Discussion

This chapter (usually chapter 6 or 7, often titled Summary) must connect back to your introduction. Your stated objectives must be directly addressed here.

Thesis typeWhat this chapter must include
Research thesisResearch objectives and results; reliability and usability of results; suggestions for future research
Functional / practice-based thesisObjective of the thesis; evaluation of the development process and output; suggestions for further development
References

The reference list follows the main body text. The heading is References (style: LAB Non-numbered). The list is in alphabetical order by the first author's surname.

See the Reference List section of this guide for all formatting rules.

Appendices

Use appendices for material that supports your thesis but is too large or detailed for the main text — for example, questionnaires, interview guides, or technical drawings.

  • Each appendix has its own page numbering (not continued from the main text)
  • Appendices are listed below the table of contents — not inside it
  • If an appendix is confidential, it is not included in the published version

3Academic Language

These are the key language rules for your thesis. Good academic writing is clear, direct, and easy to read.

Language Quality

Ask yourself these questions when you proofread:

  • Is every sentence necessary?
  • Can I make this sentence shorter without losing meaning?
  • Am I using professional vocabulary for my field?
  • Am I using direct, active constructions?
❌ AvoidAn evaluation of the results was carried out by the research team within the framework of the project.
✅ PreferThe research team evaluated the results.
Common problems to avoid
  • Informal words: a lot of, kind of, big, good, great
  • Vague filler phrases: within the framework of, in terms of, in the context of
  • Long noun phrases: the evaluation of the results was carried out → just evaluated
  • Sentences that are too long — aim for a maximum of about 30–40 words per sentence
Grammatical Person & Voice

Use the third person in most of your thesis. This keeps the focus on the research, not the researcher.

Good subject choices for your sentences
This thesis focuses on the impact of digital marketing on consumer behaviour.
Chapter 3 introduces the theoretical framework.
The respondents indicated a strong preference for digital channels.
The findings show that customer loyalty depends on more than price.
Passive vs. Active Voice
VoiceWhen to use itExample
PassiveWhen the action matters more than who did it; to maintain a formal toneData were collected through semi-structured interviews.
ActiveFor clearer, shorter sentences; often preferred in qualitative researchI collected data through semi-structured interviews.
💡
Using "I" is acceptable in many academic contexts. Discuss with your supervisor — conventions vary by field.
Numbers
RuleExample
Spell out numbers zero to nineWe interviewed five respondents.
Use numerals for 10 and aboveThe survey was completed by 23 participants.
Never start a sentence with a numeral — spell it out or rewrite the sentenceTwenty-two out of 35 respondents said…
Always use numerals with the % symbol. No space between the number and %.Approximately 60% of respondents…
Use numerals for precise units of measure5 kg, 2 cm, 6 litres
Chapter and figure references always use numeralsChapter 2 introduces… (not "Chapter two")
Percent, Percentage, Percentage Point
  • Percent — a specific number out of 100: 60%
  • Percentage — the concept, or a general amount: The percentage of respondents increased.
  • Percentage point — the difference between two percentages: If a rate goes from 10% to 15%, it increased by 5 percentage points (not "5 percent").
Contractions & Acronyms
No contractions in academic writing
ContractionWrite this instead
don'tdo not
it'sit is
can'tcannot (one word)
won'twill not
haven'thave not
we'rewe are
Acronym rules
  • Introduce first: write the full term with the acronym in brackets — The World Health Organization (WHO) reported… — then use WHO throughout.
  • Avoid short forms in the main text: write et cetera, information, statistics — not etc., info, stats.
  • Latin abbreviations (i.e., e.g., et al.) are accepted mainly inside brackets: (e.g., age, gender, and income)
  • Standard units (kg, cm, min) are always abbreviated when used with a numeral.
Avoiding Emotive Language

Academic writing reports facts and analyses evidence. Emotional or opinion words reduce your credibility. Replace them with precise, factual language.

AvoidUse instead
The results were greatThe results were significant
A huge increase…A 25% increase… (be specific)
The famous study by X…The study by X… (fame is not relevant)
I believe / feel / think that…The data suggest that… / The findings show that…
This is bad because…This is problematic because…
💡
When you need to evaluate a source, use academic words: robust, limited, comprehensive, reliable, promising.
Sentence Types
TypeUse in thesis?Note
Declarative (statements)Yes — this is the main typeFive respondents were interviewed.
QuestionsMostly avoidAvoid rhetorical questions. Use a direct statement instead.
Imperative (commands/instructions)Limited useAcceptable in questionnaire instructions or parenthetical signposting: (see Figure 1)
ExclamatoryNeverNo exclamation marks in academic writing
Sentence Structure Types
TypePatternExample
SimpleOne independent clauseFive respondents were interviewed.
CompoundTwo independent clauses joined by ; or FANBOYS (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so)They interviewed five respondents, and they recorded the discussion.
ComplexOne independent + one dependent clause (because, while, if, when, although…)Because the data were insufficient, the study recommends further research.
Compound-complexTwo independent clauses + one dependent clauseThey interviewed the respondents as a group because they wanted to examine collaboration, and they recorded the interactions for analysis.
💡
When a dependent clause starts the sentence, use a comma: "Because the data were insufficient, the study recommends further investigation."
Lists

Use lists where they genuinely help clarity. But a thesis must be built on paragraphs — lists cannot replace them.

List rules
  • Every list needs an introductory sentence or phrase before the bullets
  • No commas after bullets
  • All bullet items must be grammatically parallel — all noun phrases, or all verb phrases, etc.
  • If bullets are not full sentences: start lowercase, full stop after the last bullet only
  • If bullets are full sentences: capital letter and full stop on each bullet
❌ Commas after bulletsThe system has several features:
• Fast processing,
• Secure login,
• Easy export.
✅ CorrectThe system has several features:
• fast processing
• secure login
• easy export.

4Paragraphs & Cohesion

Every paragraph needs a clear structure. Your writing also needs to flow smoothly from one idea to the next. This section explains how.

Paragraph Structure

Every paragraph in your thesis should follow a three-part structure:

PartWhat it does
Topic sentenceStates the main idea of the paragraph. Put the most important information first.
Supporting sentencesExplain the main idea. Provide evidence, examples, and citations. Explain why the evidence matters.
Concluding sentenceLinks back to the main idea and/or forward to the next paragraph.
Example paragraph — with annotations

TOPICDifferentiation serves as a driver for Marimekko, which allows the company to adjust pricing based on consumer psychology.

SUPPORTHuefner and Sanders (2012, 27) define differentiation as a tactic where prices are set according to a customer's perceived ability to pay. To achieve this, Marimekko uses multi-channel surveys to gather consumer insights, which then inform specific product and pricing adjustments (Jones 2023a, 19–21). These insights are vital because they ensure that the company's premium positioning remains aligned with actual market demand.

CLOSEBy bridging the gap between consumer expectations and price, Marimekko's differentiation strategy serves as a foundational component of its wider corporate goals.

The next paragraph starts: "Beyond pricing tactics, Marimekko's wider goals…" — it picks up the phrase "wider goals" from the closing sentence. This is how paragraphs link together.

Cohesion

Cohesion connects your writing. Use these four techniques:

1. Transition words (signposting)

Words like however, consequently, and furthermore show the logical connection between ideas. See the Signposting section for a full list.

2. Repeat key terms — do not use synonyms

Use the same word for the same concept throughout — switching to a synonym suggests you mean something different.

❌ Confusingdifferentiation → strategy → approach → mechanism
✅ Cleardifferentiation → differentiation strategy → Marimekko's differentiation
3. Use "this" and "these" carefully

These pronouns are good for linking sentences — but make sure the reader knows what they refer to. If there is any doubt, add the noun: this strategy, these findings.

❌ UnclearThe researcher evaluated the survey, the interviews, and the case study. This was used as the basis for the methodology chapter.
✅ ClearThe researcher evaluated the survey, the interviews, and the case study. This triangulated data was used as the basis for the methodology chapter.
4. Known-New: linking sentences with passive voice

In English, new information comes at the end of a sentence and known (given) information comes at the start. The passive voice lets you move known information to the front of the next sentence.

Inbound marketing builds brand awareness.    ← "brand awareness" is new
Brand awareness can be defined as customers' ability to recognise a brand.  ← moved to front
Signposting

Signposting words guide the reader through your argument. Follow the punctuation rule for all of them.

⚠️
Punctuation rule:
Two sentences: The theory is popular. However, the data is limited.
One sentence: The theory is popular; however, the data is limited.

Use a comma after the signposting word at the start of a new sentence. Use a semicolon before + comma after when joining two clauses into one sentence.
➕Adding information
Furthermore,In addition,Moreover,Additionally,Similarly,
"Furthermore, recent studies suggest that customer loyalty is driven by brand values."
"The strategy reduced costs; moreover, it improved employee satisfaction."
⇄Comparing & contrasting
However,Conversely,Nevertheless,In contrast,On the contrary,
"The results supported the hypothesis; however, the second trial yielded conflicting data."
"Large corporations benefit from economies of scale. In contrast, small companies rely on flexibility."
➜Cause & effect
Consequently,Therefore,As a result,Hence,Accordingly,
"The company failed to adapt to digital trends; consequently, market share declined."
🔎Giving examples
For instance,For example,Specifically,To illustrate,In particular,
"Several companies have adopted circular economy principles. For example, IKEA now offers a buy-back scheme."
🏁Concluding
In conclusion,To summarise,In short,Overall,
"Overall, the study demonstrates that early stakeholder engagement significantly reduces project risk."
💡
Use at most one signposting word per paragraph. Too many makes your writing feel forced and can obscure your argument.
Punctuation

General rule: no space before a punctuation mark, but a space after.

Comma
  • In a list: The respondents were asked about their breakfast, lunch, and dinner.
  • After an introductory element: First, the respondents answered questions about their eating habits.
  • After a dependent clause that starts the sentence: Because the results were inconclusive, some respondents were asked to return.
  • Before a final phrase: Few respondents explained their dietary habits in detail, specifically regarding protein intake.
Semicolon
  • To link two closely related sentences (where a full stop would feel too final): The respondents reflected on their habits; this helped get an overview of how they view food.
  • To link contrasting ideas: The diet was not about weight loss; it was about heart health.
  • To separate complex list items that already contain commas: …leafy, green vegetables; lean, organic proteins; and fresh, seasonal fruits.
Colon
  • Before a list: The respondents came from three cities: Helsinki, Espoo, and Lahti.
  • To point to an explanation or conclusion: The policy has one clear aim: to reduce waste by 40%.
Apostrophe
  • Possession (singular): the company's strategy
  • Possession (plural): the companies' strategies
  • its = possessive (no apostrophe): The company changed its strategy.
  • it's = it is (with apostrophe) — but do not use contractions in academic writing
  • Dates: in the 1990s (no apostrophe — this is just a plural)

5Verb Tenses

Different parts of your thesis use different tenses. The tense you choose signals why you are writing — to report a fact, describe a past study, or interpret a finding.

Quick Reference
1 · Introduction
Present topic & aims  Future / Present chapter roadmap
2 · Theoretical Background
Past specific studies  Present author arguments  Present Perfect research trends
3 · Empirical Part
Past dominant — all methods and results
4 · Discussion
Past referring to findings  Present interpreting meaning
5 · Conclusion
Present main points  Future / Modal recommendations
🕐 Interactive Tense Guide →
By Thesis Section
Introduction — Present tense

Your topic, aims, and context are still true when the reader reads — use present tense.

This thesis examines digital literacy among first-year university students.
The study aims to identify key barriers to academic writing.

For the chapter roadmap, both future and present tense are correct — just be consistent:

Future — correctChapter 2 will present the theoretical framework.
Present — also correctChapter 2 presents the theoretical framework.
⚠️
Do not write "This thesis aimed to…" in the introduction. Save past tense for the conclusion, where you look back at what you did.
Theoretical Background — Three tenses

This section uses three tenses. You can use all three in the same paragraph — this is normal.

TenseWhen to use itExample
PastDescribing a specific completed study — usually when the year appears with the author's nameSmith (2018) found that motivation declined in online courses.
PresentThe author's claim or argument — still relevant todaySmith (2018) argues that autonomy plays a central role in learning.
Present PerfectA research trend that started in the past and is still ongoingResearchers have increasingly focused on the role of peer feedback (Smith 2018; Johnson 2020).
ℹ️
It is correct to mix all three tenses in one paragraph: "Smith (2018) found [past] that motivation declined — and this remains [present] a key issue today, as more recent studies have confirmed [present perfect]."
Empirical Part — Past tense

Your research is complete — use past tense for both methods and results.

Data were collected through semi-structured interviews with 12 participants.
The analysis revealed three recurring themes across the data.
💡
The one exception: use present tense when you refer to a table or figure — "Table 1 shows the results."
Discussion — Two tenses
Past — referring to findingsThe results indicated a preference for written feedback.
Participants reported feeling unsupported.
Present — interpreting meaningThis suggests that feedback timing matters.
The pattern aligns with what Smith (2018) found.
✅
Common pattern: "The interviews showed that students had problems with citations [past] — this suggests that more support is needed [present]."
Conclusion — Present + Future / Modal
The findings demonstrate the value of formative feedback in academic writing. ← present tense
Future studies could explore the long-term effects of peer feedback. ← modal verb

A common pattern: "This thesis aimed to… and found that… These results suggest that…"

Key Rules
✅ Mixing tenses is normal

When your purpose changes, the tense changes too. Reporting a result (past) and interpreting it (present) are different actions — changing tense is correct, not inconsistent.

⚠️ Changing tense without reason is a problem

Before changing tense, ask: Why? If you have no reason, stay in the same tense. Random changes make your writing hard to follow.

🎓 Conventions vary by field

These patterns follow standard academic English. Always check with your supervisor — conventions in science subjects may differ from those in business or the social sciences.

6Sources & Citations

You must cite all sources you use. This section explains which sources are acceptable and how to cite them correctly.

Finding Good Sources — The CRAAP Test

Before you use a source, check these five things:

LetterWhat to check
C — CurrencyWhen was it published? Is it recent enough for your topic?
R — RelevanceDoes it directly address your research question?
A — AuthorityWho is the author? Is the publication peer-reviewed?
A — AccuracyIs the information supported by evidence? Are sources cited?
P — PurposeWhy was it written? To inform, or to sell something?
Sources to avoid
  • Commercial blogs and company websites — these aim to promote a product, not provide reliable information
  • Dictionaries and encyclopaedias (including Wikipedia) — useful for getting started, but not for academic citation
  • Pirated e-books — if you find a full book as a PDF online, check that it has an open-access licence. If not, find it through LAB Primo instead
  • Pages with no named author where an author would normally be expected

For help finding quality sources: LUT/LAB library information retrieval guide →

In-Text Citation Rules
SituationRuleExample
One sentenceFull stop after the bracketGood management helps employees develop competence (Ekholm & Salmenkangas 2015, 35–36).
Multiple sentencesFull stop inside the bracketBrand loyalty develops over time. It is closely linked to trust. (Korhonen 2020, 58.)
Multiple sourcesChronological order, separated by semicolons(Siukosaari 2015, 205; Ojanen 2016, 18; Kuutti 2017, 102.)
Two authorsUse & — never "and"(Chang & Nguyen 2011, 13.)
Three or more authorsFirst author + et al. — in the text only(Jones et al. 2009, 43–45.)
Narrative citation — year + pagePut year and page directly after the author's name, not at the end of the sentenceMyers and Kassing (2009, 72) claim that…

et al. is not allowed in the reference list. You can use it in in-text citations, but all authors must be named in full in the references list.
Weak vs. Strong Author Citations

Every in-text citation can be written in two ways. Your choice signals whether you want to emphasise the author or the information.

Weak / ParentheticalStrong / Narrative
StructureAuthor is in the brackets: (Smith 2021, 45)Author is in the sentence: Smith (2021, 45) argues that…
Best forMost paraphrased passages; when the information matters more than the sourceDefinitions; comparing two authors' views; crediting a framework or theory
EffectSmoother flow; more objective feelSource is immediately visible; makes evidence easy to identify
RequiresNothing extraA reporting verb or according to
Weak: Differentiation is a tactic where prices reflect a customer's perceived ability to pay (Huefner & Sanders 2012, 27).
Strong — definition: Huefner and Sanders (2012, 27) define differentiation as a tactic where prices reflect a customer's perceived ability to pay.
Strong — contrast: Jones (2023b, 25) notes that sustainable growth is the main goal; however, Huefner and Sanders (2012, 86) point out that brand licensing brings equal returns.
💡
Use both styles — do not rely on one throughout.

Reporting Verbs

The verb you choose in a narrative citation signals your attitude to the source's claim — not all verbs mean the same thing.

Tentative — the author is cautious or unsure
suggesthypothesiseimplyproposespeculatepostulate
Smith (2021) suggests that remote work may improve productivity in certain contexts.
Neutral — reporting facts, data, or methods without adding opinion
describeshowrevealnoteindicatereportobservestateexaminefind
The study shows a significant increase in urban migration.
Strong — the author makes a firm, confident claim
argueclaimemphasisecontendmaintainassertrefutechallengedeny
Miller (2020) argues that the current economic model is unsustainable.
Grammar rules
❌ Missing -s (third person)Smith argue that… / The study show that…
✅ CorrectSmith argues that… / The study shows that…
💡
Use present tense (argues, shows, notes) to show the source is still relevant today. Use past tense (found, reported) when treating a study as a completed event — especially when the year appears next to the author's name: "Smith (2018) found that…"

Direct Quotations

A direct quotation reproduces the source's exact words. Use them only when the exact wording matters — for example, for a legal definition or a key term you are analysing. In most cases, paraphrase instead.

At LAB, do not use quotation marks. Use italics instead.

Short quotation — inside the sentence

Integrate the quotation naturally into your sentence. Use italics. No quotation marks.

Bryson (1990, 240) claims that the countries where strife and violence have been most pronounced are the ones where minority languages have been most strenuously suppressed.
Long quotation — block format

Separate from the body text with blank lines before and after. Indent to the next tab stop. Use italics. No quotation marks. Put the reference in brackets after the text.

U.S. English and other such groups maintain that linguistic divisions have caused unrest in several countries, such as Canada and Belgium – though they generally fail to note that the countries where strife and violence have been most pronounced are the ones where minority languages have been most strenuously suppressed. (Bryson 1990, 240.)

7Reference List

Your reference list includes every source you cited in the thesis — nothing more, nothing less. It follows the main body text under the heading References (style: LAB Non-numbered).

Rules & Format
  • Order: alphabetical A–Z by first author's surname
  • Every cited source must appear; no uncited sources may appear
  • Authors: Surname, Initial(s).
  • Multiple authors joined with & in the same order as in the original source
  • et al. is not allowed — all authors must be named in full
  • Same author, same year: add lowercase letters — Jones 2023a, Jones 2023b
  • No full stop after a URL or DOI
  • Always include a retrieval date for digital sources
  • If access requires login or payment: write Limited availability at instead of Available at

Secondary Source — What goes in the list?

If you cite a source through another author's work, only the source you actually read goes in the reference list.

In-text: (Drucker 1954, according to Hamel 2007, 89.)
Reference list: Hamel 2007 only — not Drucker.

8Visuals

Tables, figures, and images can help the reader understand your research — but only if you follow the rules. A visual that is not discussed in the text should not be in your thesis.

Types & Numbering

Language & Style

Choose reporting verbs carefully. "Argue" signals a contested opinion. If the source is just reporting data or findings, use a neutral verb.

❌ Poor✅ Good

Avoid over-complicated or overly passive constructions. Prefer clear, direct phrasing.

❌ Poor✅ Good

Avoid direct quotations unless the exact wording is essential. Paraphrasing shows that you understood the source. When you do quote, use italics — not quotation marks.

❌ Poor✅ Good

Short quotation not in italics.Short quotations inside a sentence must be in italics: The Act provides that every person is entitled to health care required by his state of health (Act on the Status and Rights of Patients 785/1992, Section 3.)

Do not use scare quotes to highlight a key term or expression. Putting quotation marks around a term to signal that it is important, specialised, or borrowed is an informal habit that has no place in academic writing. If a term needs introducing, define it in a sentence. If convention requires marking the first use of a technical term, use italics — once, at the point of introduction.

⚠️
Scare quotes can also signal that you are distancing yourself from a term without explaining why. Readers expect you to either commit to the term and define it, or choose a different one. Quotation marks are not a substitute for a clear explanation.
❌ Poor✅ Good
The company used a "lean" approach to reduce waste. (scare quotes around a key term)The company adopted a lean approach, a production philosophy focused on eliminating non-value-adding activities (Womack & Jones 1996, 15).
Participants reported feeling "burned out" after the restructuring. (scare quotes around a borrowed expression)Participants reported symptoms consistent with burnout, including emotional exhaustion and reduced personal accomplishment (Maslach & Leiter 2016, 103).
The model relies on so-called "big data" to generate predictions. (scare quotes combined with hedging)The model relies on large-scale datasets — commonly referred to as big data — to generate predictions.
