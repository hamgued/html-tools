An in-text citation can be weak (parenthetical) — author and year tucked inside brackets — or strong (narrative) — the author's name woven into the sentence. Click Next step to reveal each citation, see what type it is, and learn when to use it.

ℹ️ About the terminology. "Weak" and "strong" are classroom labels used here for clarity. Major style guides use different terms for the same distinction: parenthetical / narrative (APA), or non-integral / integral (Hyland). The mechanics are identical — only the names differ.

Example paragraph

Marimekko's pricing strategy
A student paragraph using both citation styles. Reveal one step at a time.
step 0 / 3

Differentiation serves as a driver for Marimekko, which allows the company to adjust pricing based on consumer psychology. Huefner and Sanders (2012, 27) define differentiation as a tactic where prices are set according to a customer's perceived ability to pay. To achieve this, Marimekko uses multi-channel surveys to gather consumer insights, which then inform specific product and pricing adjustments (Jones 2023a, 19–21). These insights are vital because they ensure that the company's premium positioning remains aligned with actual market demand. By bridging the gap between consumer expectations and price, Marimekko's differentiation strategy serves as a foundational component of its wider corporate goals.

Beyond pricing tactics, Marimekko's wider goals focus on sustainable international growth in the global textile market (Marimekko 2022). Jones (2023b, 25) notes that focusing on sustainable international growth… However, Huefner and Sanders (2012, 86) point out that…

Strong (narrative)
Commonly used for definitions.
The author's name is part of the sentence: Huefner and Sanders (2012, 27) define X as…
Weak (parenthetical)
The basic style for in-text citations.
Use this when you just need to credit the source without naming the author in your sentence.
Strong (narrative)
Commonly used for comparing different authors' ideas.
Jones (2023b, 25) notes A… However, Huefner and Sanders (2012, 86) point out B…
💡 Quick rule: If you're defining a term or comparing what authors say, prefer narrative (strong). If you're just supporting a claim, parenthetical (weak) is the default.

A citation can refer to one sentence or to several sentences. The position of the period changes accordingly. When several sources back the same idea, they can be grouped inside the same parentheses.

Example paragraph

Marimekko's brand licensing
Watch how punctuation shifts depending on citation range, and how grouped sources are formatted.
step 0 / 2

Marimekko leverages strategic brand licensing to amplify its market presence. Licensing functions as a low-risk entry mechanism into non-core categories (e.g., tableware and home technology); this allows the brand to monetize its iconic print library without heavy capital investment (Jones 2021, 5). This approach relies on aesthetic consistency, where Marimekko's Unikko pattern maintains brand equity even when applied to third-party manufacturing. By carefully selecting partners that mirror its values, Marimekko ensures these collaborations drive both high-margin royalty revenue and global brand awareness. Consequently, this model transforms the company's artistic intellectual property into a scalable financial asset. (Marimekko 2022; Jones et al. 2023, 15; Smith 2024; Henderson 2026, 6.)

One-sentence range
Citation is inside the sentence.
A period is placed after the closing parenthesis: … investment (Jones 2021, 5).
Several-sentence range
Citation is outside the sentences.
The period goes inside the parentheses. Multiple sources are:
  • listed in chronological order (oldest → newest)
  • separated with semicolons
⚠️ Watch the period. Where the full stop sits is the single clearest signal of whether your citation refers to one sentence or to a whole block. Misplacing it changes the meaning of your citation.
ℹ️ Why chronological? Listing grouped sources from oldest to newest helps the reader trace how an idea has developed over time in the literature.
